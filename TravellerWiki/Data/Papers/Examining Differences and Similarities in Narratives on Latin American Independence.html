Tutino’s edited volume, New Countries (2016) and Blumenthal’s book Exile and Nation-State Formation in Argentina (2019) both discuss the topic of nation-state formation in post-independence Central and South America; however, the two books take different approaches to exploring this topic. This paper will discuss some of the similarities and differences in the approaches taken in the aforementioned books. The books broadly differ in three areas: the area of the topic being covered – Blumenthal focuses on Argentina and Chile whereas Tutino takes a broader look at all of South and Central America; the approach taken for that topic – Blumenthal presents a social history focusing on several members of the intellectual elite who went into exile while Tutino provides a more general national history; finally, the choice of who to focus on as the ‘protagonists’ of the narrative – Blumenthal uses a recurring ‘cast’ of exiles who are discussed in each chapter, while the sections of Tutino’s volume discuss a variety of figures, usually focusing on those linked to key events in that nation’s history.

Tutino’s edited sections provide a general history for the countries of Central and South America, from Mexico to the Andean heartland and further south. Its primary focus is the period of independence, providing the national history for each nation or region that it discusses. An example of this is the background in the chapter: From one Patria, Two Nations in the Andean Heartland where Chambers discusses the history of the Andean peoples gathered around Lake Titicaca, from their start “almost four thousand years ago”1 to the eventual creation of Peru and Bolivia, and how the creation of those nations inadvertently prevented the creation of a united Andean heartland. To explain how that transition happens, authors in various sections provide a broad national history, discussing topics from key military victories and revolutions, important social players, and movements, to the economy and the role that the transition to industrial capitalism played in the formation of these nation-states. An example of this is in the chapter Becoming Mexico, wherein the vitality of Mexico to the world economy is made readily apparent, as its silver was part of what “stimulated the global trades for centuries”.2 The chapter later discusses how a drop in the global silver trade due to Napoleon’s destruction of the Spanish empire coincides with the Mexican economy’s downfall, and the empire’s collapse created space for independence, which would only be stabilized decades after it was initially achieved, in part thanks to funding from British banks and investors in exchange for low tariffs and recognition as a country,3 showcasing the connections between industrial capitalism and national development. Tutino’s sections also provide a variety of stories focusing on more minor individuals related to the nations they’re discussing, such as the important uprisings led by Túpac Amaru,4 but also others who worked alongside the well-known events and leaders, such as Tupaj Katari who was fighting a similar fight as Túpac Amaru during the same period.5 Thus the sections of New Countries present various nations’ histories, through a broad overview of different factors while examining different actors who played a role in each nation’s history, rarely having a person who appears in multiple chapters outside of key figures for the entire continent like Simón Bolívar.

In contrast, Blumenthal’s Exile and Nation-State Formation in Argentina and Chile provides a focused look at the impact exiles had on the formation of nations in South America, especially Argentina and Chile. The book does not focus on the national narrative of what occurred in these countries, or the events leading up to or after independence; instead, it focuses on the exile experience, the jobs exiles worked, and the role exiles played in the creation of new countries which they saw themselves as citizens of, and in some cases would become the future leaders of.6 Because of this focus on the exile experience the book does not discuss the larger economies of Chile or Argentina; instead it focuses on the local economies that allowed exiles to stay employed and spread their ideas. For example, the third chapter discusses the way that existing colonial-commercial networks were used to spread political texts and act as commercial enterprises for some thinkers in exile, detailing how J. M. Gutierrez was key in the creation of a thriving literary exchange among various exiles across Central and South America.7 This example is emblematic of the entire book, as it introduces several key ‘characters’ in chapter one, and then goes on to often re-reference them later throughout various chapters in the construction of a larger narrative: the exile narrative and the personal story for each of the discussed exiles. Whether talking about the familial relationships and how some families had developed a history of exile, with multiple generations having gone into exile, such as the Bilbao family,8 or the painful loneliness of being an exile, the book’s primary focus is on exiles and their impact in the creation of new republics out of the countries they initially fled, not on the overarching history of those countries.

Comparing the two books, there are some key similarities and differences between them. The largest differences between the two are in scope and scale, while the largest similarity is demonstrating the interconnectivity of the emerging countries in the period after independence in South American history.

When looking at the scope of the two books, the largest difference becomes glaringly obvious: Tutino’s book covers a wide spectrum of countries, whereas Blumenthal is focusing on Argentina and Chile, with mentions of other countries and the role they played in the exile life, such as Peru, Uruguay, and Bolivia. As well, the scope of what history itself is covered is different in the books, with each section of Tutino’s work discussing a different nation and providing its history from pre-colonization through to the early post-independence period, and while tying its history into a series of established themes, like the silver trade and slavery. Blumenthal, by contrast focuses on the exile experience and uses various events throughout the post-independence period as springboards for further study of various exiles and their actions, such as referencing events that caused new waves of exiles, or the revolutions that brought them back to their homes. In this same vein is the scale of what history is covered in the two books, with the nature of each topic’s impact being the degree to which it effects the greater world. The exiles’ impact on the development of various republican institutions, while important, has a smaller effect on the whole of South America, whereas the scale of various national histories is quite grand, but the specifics of each are less clear. For example, it is shown in Blumenthal how exiles helped in creating a national spirit for freedom of the press,9 a relatively small part of the greater national idea, versus Tutino discussing the vitality of the silver trade to the global economy shows the difference in scale, i.e. social-transnational history versus global-national history.

The largest similarity between the two books is in their shared reaffirmation of the interconnectivity of the proto-nations that were forming in post-independence South America. Blumenthal demonstrates this by discussing how various exiles came to form national associations or societies. These groups would then negotiate on the international stage – sometimes acting as a government in exile - such as the Argentine Commission of Montevideo negotiating an alliance with President Rivera of Uruguay or working with the French to blockade Buenos Aires.10 Tutino’s sections, on the other hand, show their interconnectivity both through a shared narrative of liberation in Simón Bolívar, or the connectivity of slave trade; the rise of Haiti causing a decline of the sugar trade and slave importation creating a gap that Brazil was able to fill, turning it into what in British circles was known as “the slave trade personified.”11 Both books are thus able to demonstrate the interconnectivity of South America in the post-independence period: through letter writing, military campaign, economies, legal systems, history, or any other number of similarities.

Exile and Nation-State Formation in Argentina and Chile and New Countries Capitalism, Revolutions, and Nations in the Americas, 1750-1870 both offer insights into Central and South America in the period after independence but do so in different ways. Where Exile provides a social history looking at the titular exiles and their experience in different countries, New Countries shows the national and international histories that led to the creation of the countries that the exiles were fleeing from, to, and helping to create. They do this by exploring their respective themes at different scopes and scales, while keeping the interconnectivity of the fledgling countries at the forefront of their writing.
