Changes to Political, Economic and Social Structures During the Mexican Revolution

The Mexican Revolution was a period in the early twentieth century when Mexico underwent a radical political, economic, and social transformation. This paper will trace the steps taken during the Mexican Revolution to change the political, economic, and social structures which governed Mexican life. This will be accomplished by examining each area’s structure during the Porfiriato, the transition the structure undergoes during the various revolutions and counter revolutions through to the passing of the Constitution of 1917, the enforcement of the new Constitution by President Calles, and the final revolutionary changes applied by President Cardenas.

The first structure which will be looked at is the political structure. The Porfiriato was the period of Mexican history when Porfirio Diaz was in power. Diaz claimed to be a liberal leader, but his policies pointed towards a focus on stability and order, at the expensive of actual liberal policy.1 The focus on stability was part of a plan to attract foreign businesses, a plan that also included offering special concessions to entice the foreign companies to develop in Mexico.2 These concessions helped to develop the railway network throughout Mexico, which also served as a tool to maintain order as it allowed for the quick transportation of troops to areas with worker strikes or other forms of dissent.3 A large area where Diaz’s rule was at odds with liberal policy can be seen in Diaz’s comfortable treatment of the Church, compared to the liberal anti-clerical measures which were in the 1857 Constitution.4 By and large, the political structure of the Porfiriato was one of a client-patron relationship built around the strongman Diaz.

This structure began coming apart following a TV interview wherein Diaz admitted to considering stepping down and allowing free elections.5 This inspired many different groups to begin to develop political clubs, which eventually coalesced around Madero who was advocating for “free elections and proclaimed the no-re-election principle.”6 However, during his tenure as President, Madero failed to reward the revolutionaries who had gotten him into power – either with positions in his government or with reforms that they had been asking for – and instead supported the previous elites, and promised only political reform to the people, not social or economic reform.7

Real political change came following the Constitutional Convention of 1916 and rise of President Calles. Calles hoped to prevent future elections from being as violent, allowing for a smoother transfer of power. He did this through the creation of the Patrido Nacional Revolucionario (PNR), which would pick the next president, and help them carry out their political goals by controlling politics, while keeping the political elite in power and, most importantly, letting Calles hold onto control from the background.8 It did this by “work[ing] hand and glove with the government to maintain a political elite in power through patronage and corruption, downsizing the military and controlling political opponents through electoral fraud, violence, and manipulation of the judiciary.”9

The final step in the political development of post-revolutionary Mexico was under President Cardenas. Cardenas wanted to stop being a puppet of Calles and take the Party – and nation – in a more progressive, nationalistic direction.10 To get out of his puppet status, Cardenas worked hard to build coalitions out of the various factions, which helped to centralize the government in Mexico City.11 To secure his power, Cardenas pushed for term limits in the Supreme Court, and pushed out legislators who went against his policies.12 He also reformed the PNR into the new Partido de la Revolucion Mexicana, which now gave representation to various sectors of the economy, allowing them a greater voice in national politics.13

The political structure was not the only structure which changed during the revolution to give various sectors and people a greater voice; the structure of the economy also underwent a transformation. The Porfiriato was focused on economic modernization with little care for how this was done, except for the belief that Mexico could not bring about modernization without foreign ‘help’.14 An example of this is Molina in the Yucatan, who was able to gain what was essentially a monopoly on the henequen trade through a deal made with an American company, without consideration for the loss of land of the people living there, or the greater economic ramifications of such a monopoly.15 Such consolidations of land is one of the key areas of resentment visible within the Plan de Ayala, which did not seek to abolish private property, only to distribute the holdings in a more equal way.16

Madero’s and other early revolutionary governments failure to follow through on the Plan de Ayala ultimately led to the Constitution of 1917, which included two major areas of economic reform. The first was Mexican control of its subsoil rights, advocated for by the Jacobinos based on clauses from the 1857 Constitution which had been carried forward into the new Constitution of 1917, and would eventually culminate in Article 27.17 The second was an expansion of labor rights, visible in Article 123 of the Constitution, which tasked the state with ensuring a fair wage for workers, workers’ rights in general, one day of rest per week, and other benefits.18

President Obregon expanded the governments relationship with Labor. Beginning when he was serving as a General to Carranza, Obregon used Red Battalions with union soldiers in them to defeat Pancho Villa.19 The relationship went much further during Obregon’s time as president, as he worked closely with Luis Morones, head of the Confederacion Regional de Obreros Mexicanos (CROM) – a relationship Calles would expand on by making Morones his Minister of Industry – but Morones did not look out for the workers, working instead to enrich himself and his allies.20

Greater economic reform occurred under President Cardenas. He was committed to giving land to the people – giving more than any other revolutionary leader in this period.21 He also worked with disgruntled labor leaders who had been left out of CROM, leading to the formation of the Confederacion de Trajadores Mexicanos (CTM) which limited CROM to mostly urban areas and the textile industry.22 Cardenas also expropriated the petrol industry, creating the state owned Petroleos Mexicanos.23

The social revolution was based on the anti-clerical clauses of the 1857 Constitution, and expanded on in the 1917 Constitution, which gave the state the ability to intervene in religious affairs.24 Calles pushed the religious reforms further, due to his fanatical hatred of the Roman Catholic Church.25 These reforms culminated in the Cristos Rebellion, which ended in 1929 with an agreement that the government would not enforce its anti-clerical laws in a way hostile to the Church.26

A second area of social change was education. Under Cardenas there was a large push for secular, nationalistic education. It focused on teaching mothers skills they needed to live healthier and more secular lives, but the goal “was not to emancipate women and children, but to subordinate the household to the interest of national development.”27 Initially resistant, rural towns sometimes went so far as to kill the teachers who came to them, but over time the teachers and conservative towns reached agreements to allow secular education in certain times.28 The teachers and state also worked to replace religious holidays with civic ones to help people develop a greater sense of nationalism.29

Another area of social change to facilitate the growth of nationalism was through anti-Chinese racism.30 As the Mexican government’s racial goal became one of Mestizaje, the government was able to present Chinese people as a lesser ‘other’ who were at fault for some of Mexico’s problems, there by uniting the Mexican citizens against a common enemy.31
