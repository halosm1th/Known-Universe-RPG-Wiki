“In the present social and cultural context, where there is a widespread tendency to relativize truth, practising charity in truth helps people to understand that adhering to the values of Christianity is not merely useful but essential for building a good society and for true integral human development.”1 Published in 2009, this quote from the papal encyclical Caritas In Veritate has only become more relevant as society slips further into a deeply and increasingly polarized post-truth and fake news world. In the encyclical, Pope Benedict XVI discussed a number of issues pressing both when the encyclical was written, and still so in the post COVID-19 pandemic world. This paper will reflect on some of the key issues addressed by Pope Benedict XVI, such as wealth inequality, globalization, and the development of people. It will begin with a short overview of the encyclical, followed by further analysis of each point individually, and finally summarizing the core points, connecting them, and demonstrating the value of these ideas to world leaders, young people, Catholics, Marxists, and many others in world.

Caritas in Veritate was initially written to commemorate the 40th anniversary of the Encyclical Populorum Progressio, written by Pope Paul VI, however, the 2008 financial crisis caused the encyclical to be delayed.2 The encyclical followed on Populorum Progressio’s theme of examining the Church’s social teachings, reflecting on where Populorum Progressio is still applicable and the places where the social teaching needed to be expanded on. Some of the key areas explored in the encyclical are wealth inequality, global hunger, the relationship between rights and duties, and the development of a person, connected through the concept of Charity in Truth.

The encyclical begins with a discussion on the topic of Charity in Truth, explaining how charity is at the heart of the Church’s social doctrine, and that modern societies have abandoned charity as irrelevant in interpreting and directing moral responsibility. It goes on to argue that “Truth is the light that gives meaning and value to charity.”3 This is because truth is what keeps charity from becoming sentimentality and “love becomes an empty shell, to be filled in an arbitrary way.”4

After the discussion of truth, the first chapter returns to discussing the message of Populorum Progressio, and how in an increasingly globalized world, “reason, by itself, is capable of grasping the equality between men and of giving stability to their civil coexistence, but it cannot establish fraternity.”5 It is for this reason that humanity needs the Church’s social teachings, so that a global fraternity may be established in the increasingly global world. Continuing the theme of globalization, the second chapter brings together globalization and human development, discussing the dangers of one pursuing wealth for the sake of gaining wealth, and how these themes combined work to reinforce inequality in the world. The third chapter relates the growth of wealth inequality to the lack of fraternity in the modern world, continuing on the theme of focusing on profits by advising that people push for a system which focuses not only on profits, but also on how one’s actions effect the community around them. Chapter 4 discusses the relationship between rights and responsibilities, including the responsibility to help one another, and a responsibility to the environment. The latter, the Pope reminds the reader, must be cared for by man, but the resources and environment ultimately exist to better mankind’s collective quality of life. Because of this, rich countries should be investing in renewable and more green technology so as to benefit the poorer countries, and as well, that rich countries should be working to ensure that poor countries have greater control over their resources. In chapter 5, the Pope discusses the relationship between isolation and poverty, explaining that isolation drives poverty, among other social issues, and the Pope advocates for more local charities and groups to help those in need instead of massive multinationals which take money from everywhere but produce few beneficial results for local communities, as well as advocating for a stronger United Nations that also gives a greater voice to smaller nations so as to ensure their ability to be heard. The sixth chapter discusses the role of technology in the globalized world, and it highlights two fears: the first that decisions will be made not on ethical grounds but on technical grounds, and second that as people get distilled into technical psychological beings without a soul, they begin to feel lost and empty. This paper will be focused on exploring the encyclical’s themes of wealth inequality, globalization, and the development of people.

Wealth inequality is a major issue in the world today, according to the World Inequality Report: “The poorest half of the global population barely owns any wealth at all, possessing just 2% of the total. In contrast, the richest 10% of the global population own 76% of all wealth.”6 The issue of wealth inequality is something made worse with the COVID pandemic, as the pandemic led to increased unemployment which in turn brought an increase in wealth inequality, which is compounded by inflation, an issue that is affecting the entire world as a result of supply chain issues.7 In light of these trends, the Pope’s reminder that the market “must be structured and governed in an ethical manner”8 becomes much more pressing to world leaders and young people – if we want to deal with issues like wealth inequality and improve our quality of life, we must restructure our markets to be more ethical. An example of this is Canadian garbage, which was sent to the Philippines. This act demonstrates the global nature of the world and, the lack of fraternity within it, as Canadians in a rich country shipped their garbage to a poor country rather than deal with it themselves, an unethical but potentially profitable action.9

The Pope noted that globalization could be a force for good, using global markets to help lift poor countries out of poverty. This can be seen in the reduction in global hunger and malnourishment, something which had been a great concern of Pope Benedict XVI. When Caritas In Veritate was written, there were approximately 684.4 million undernourished people; by 2019 that had dropped to 650 million, with its lowest point being 2014 with only 609.9 million undernourished people.10 However, as the Pope noted, there is rising undernourishment even among rich countries, -a trend made worse with the COVID pandemic, and which will likely continue to increase as climate change damages the environment, and wealth inequality grows.

Considering the growing wealth inequality, the encyclical reminds the reader of the relationship between rights and duties. On this topic, the Pope says: “Many people today would claim that they owe nothing to anyone except themselves. They are concerned only with their rights, and they often have great difficulty in taking responsibility for their own and other people’s integral development.”11 This is a situation which COVID has exacerbated, as the truckers protest in Canada demonstrates. Much of the rhetoric during the event was focused on one’s rights and freedoms, yet there was little discussion of one's obligations, demonstrating perfectly what Pope Benedict XVI highlighted. Pope Benedict XVI recommends that governments transition from focusing only on economic issues to also focusing on ethical issues; in Canada this can be seen with the rise in young people pushing for an ethical governmental relationship with First Nations people built on the ethics-based idea of reconciliation. The Pope warned of the government not acting morally but rather following the advice of technocrats acting without ethical consideration, a Canadian example of which is the Quebec government toying with the idea of taxing the unvaccinated.12 Although one may have a social obligation to get vaccinated, that does not make it ethical for the government to coerce the unwilling into getting vaccinated, even if from a purely technical perspective, it is better that more people are vaccinated.

According to the Pope, another aspect of the purely technical perspective is “the tendency to consider the problems and emotions of the interior life from a purely psychological point of view.”13 This view, combined with a lack of fraternity as discussed by the Pope, may be part of what is driving the division in modern society – people turn their opposition into a set of psychological groupings, and those with whom they disagree are made incapable of developing due to those same groupings, as our ethical drive is “often overwhelmed or supressed by ethical and cultural considerations of individualistic and utilitarian nature.”14 With our suppressed ethical drives, in the increasingly globalized but isolated world, “the soul feels abandoned,”15 while the person grows depressed from the lack of fraternity; they channel their emotions into anger, placing blame for their isolation on others, worsening their mental health. This is another area COVID has made worse, as increased isolation from lockdowns, combined with the increasing inflation, drives the prices of mental health treatment higher, highlighting another form of inequality between those who can get mental health treatments and those left to suffer on their own.16

As we recover from the COVID pandemic, it is worthwhile for world leaders, youth, government employees, Catholics, and others in society to reflect on Charity in Truth. The rising inflation will only increase the inequality in wealth, the changing environment will make global undernourishment a greater challenge, and as a result, people will become more tribal and utilitarian. It is for these reasons that popes’ writings are valuable to world leaders, young people demanding change, and anyone else worried about the future of humanity. The concept of fraternity, and its role in the development of mentally and physically healthy people and the communities that nurture them is worthy of deeper consideration. As well, the restructuring of free markets to take into consideration ethical matters, and then using that ethical market to reduce wealth inequality between rich and poor countries, and between the rich and poor within those countries, is something world leaders and the young should be considering as they transition to a post-COVID world.
