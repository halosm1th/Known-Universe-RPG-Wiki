Christianity was winning the battle for control over the Roman Empire by the end of the fourth century. However, it was struggling to take religious control over the province of North Africa, whose wide range of religious diversity helped to play a vital role in Christianity’s, and Roman Catholicism’s, process of defining itself: defining itself as separate from the Jewish religion from which it came, from the other Christian sects in North Africa and elsewhere who challenged the authority of the Empire-aligned Catholic Church, and from the pagans who had been the foundation of the Empire for most of its life. To see how Christianity was able to define itself in this way, this paper will examine the relationship between the Jewish and Christian people in North Africa and the Jewish heritage visible in places in Ethiopia far into the future; the role Donatists played in helping to define the Catholic church as separate from their own, while also playing a role in merging the Catholic Church and Roman States; and finally examining the Catholic view of pagan religions and how the Universal Church encouraged pagans to embrace and take pride in their local culture instead of the dominant Roman culture of the Empire.

What denotes the difference between a Christian and a Jew in North Africa? Examining artifacts in the modern day can make it difficult to differentiate,1 and it is not made any easier by places like Ethiopia, whose Christian faith had a strong Judaic twist to it.2 Artifacts can be especially difficult to place, as both Christians and Jewish groups shared many stories, such as the sacrifice of Isaac, but were often associated with Christianity, which can embellish the perception of popular belief.3 This is made more challenging when considering that a person in North Africa may have had many ‘identities’ which made up the person: “one woman might identify herself as a Jew while entering a synagogue, as a Roman of the provinces when participating in Roman law courts, and as a Punic-speaker in the marketplace.”4 As well, such a person with a multitude of identities may even have embraced more than one concept of divinity.5 These challenges are compounded by the lack of standardized thought even among religions, such as Judaism, as the culture of Jews in North Africa was different than that of those in Asia Minor or Rome.6 When all this is combined with the specter of time, the end result is a mess of identity and religion that lacked a clear boundary between Roman, Jewish, North African, and even Christian to an extent. This Christian-Jewish mix could be seen prominently in Ethiopia.

While in other parts of the Empire the Jewish-Christian schism may have been clear due to its own self-imposition,7 this was not the case in Ethiopia, where instead Christians maintained a strong Jewish heritage far into the future. An obvious example of this would be the Ethiopian practice of recognizing the Sabbath8, and its eventually attempted forced conversion to Catholicism in the 17th century.9 Throughout North Africa, Christianity found a stronger “regional influence than Graeco-Roman influence”10 and was less effected by the schism and its future Hellenistic beliefs, instead developing to care more about its own traditions.11 This shared focus on traditions and ideas helped to create what St. Augustine would call a “Unity” separate from the Catholic Church, instead composed of “Heretics, Jews, and pagans”12 which seemed to encompass much of Roman North African. But what about the Christian heretics who St. Augustine is speaking of?

After the last of Diocletian’s Great Persecutions several new Christian sects arose, and by 451 the Copts of Egypt had had enough and rejected the Orthodoxy which had been imposed on them,13 as they feared the role religion was taking on in society.14 The Coptics, like the Donatists, rejected the claim that the Emperors’ authority could unite the Church,15 which in practice had become true as North Africa had begun to drift away from the Empire into its own sphere.16 This stood in opposition to the Catholics who saw it as their Emperor-sanctioned duty to create a Universal church.17 Neither side was truly in a commanding position though, as seen during the Conference of 411, where it was reported that there were 284 Donatist bishops and 286 Catholic bishops,18 which shows just how little control the Catholics actively had over the area. Towns were happy to switch between faiths, especially from Donatist to Catholic, so long as they were given a local bishop instead of a priest;19 this was difficult for the Catholic Church which struggled to recruit clergy members and was thus often scraping the bottom of the proverbial barrel.20 When “bishop bribing” failed, the Empire employed forms of repression such as banning from employment.21 While initially not forced to convert, this would change in 405 when the Donatists were denied their church, leading to some false conversions;22 for some Catholics however it was seen as a rightful continuation of Roman Citizenship – to be a full Roman Citizen, one must practice the correct religion23 - while some, like St. Augustine, may have believed this practice was fair, on the ground the Emperors’ lack of control often meant that enforcement fell to the locals, and how fair that was is not as clear.24

One of the key areas of difference between Donatists and Catholics is the importance placed on scripture, especially the literal scripts and books from which the holy stories came. For the Donatists, the internalization of scripture was a grand assertion of belief.25 This is because a common belief in the Antiquity period was that letters, books, etc. often contained the ‘spirit’ of the author,26 and that destroying Holy Books, as had been ordered previously, was tantamount to destroying God.27 Of course, Catholics like St. Augustine did not deny the value of scripture, even seeing the healing powers of the object as superior to that of an amulet,28 they simply favoured religious unity, hoping to restore the Empire while continuing the work of previous Emperors.29 However, due to the Emperors’ lack of control over North Africa, and the high degree of religious diversity, the bishops were given a large degree of power and leeway to ensure that the law was enforced and unity brought about.30

But this idea of unity within the Roman Empire from the Emperor was offensive to some, especially those who would have once been the dominant religious group in the Empire: pagans. The pagan Maximus, for example argued with St. Augustine over the value of the ”civic cults of his colonia.”31 For this pagan at least, there was not a denial of the Christian God as a god, even the highest god;32 instead Maximus’ argument rests on the claim that multiple different deities could offer a route to the highest god.33 As well, for Maximus the forum was home to the old, Roman, gods; to become Christian meant abandoning them and the whole of Roman divinity and culture, something Maximus was against.34 For Augustine there was a different outcome which the pagan could embrace. Maximus could leave behind the old Roman gods and instead embrace his own, African, culture and literature.35 For most pagans, tradition was extremely important, and a movement swept over the Empire of educated Romans debating the value of traditional religion against the new encroaching religion, couching their discussions in literary works and great thinkers of the past like St. Augustine and Maximus had done in their discussion.36

North Africa, on the opposite side of the Mediterranean from what had once been the heart of the Roman Empire, was now becoming harder and harder for Emperors to maintain their control over. This was only made more difficult by the religious differences present in the region, but it had many effects on Christianity: it challenged the belief that Christianity was separate from Judaism, as seen in Ethiopia and the makeup of the people on the ground; it was challenged by the Donatists and Coptics, whose unwillingness to bend to the Emperor combined with popular support forced the Church to assume a more controlling role in the region; meanwhile the educated debated religion using traditional language and concepts, forcing those who would debate them back to be educated in the Old World, thus preserving some amount of those traditions.
