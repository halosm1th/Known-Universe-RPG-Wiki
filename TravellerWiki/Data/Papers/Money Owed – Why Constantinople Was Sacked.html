Money Owed – Why Constantinople Was Sacked

by

Thomas McIntosh

April 4, 2022

1981 Words

Dr. Jason Brown

HIST 2420

University of Manitoba

The Crusades were a sanguinary enterprise, and the motivations of those who went to fight were many. Some joined out of devotion to the Church, others out of a desire for wealth, or for any number of other reasons which depended upon who that person was, where they were born, what had occurred during their life, etc. For those in Venice when word reached of the calling of a Fourth Crusade, their responses were varied. In the time since then, because of the horrors brought by the Fourth Crusade, their reasons for crusading have come under scrutiny. From one point of view, the Venetians were capitalists seeking to make a profit on devout crusaders, whose devotion was exploited by the Venetians to settled old scores and expand their Empire through the destruction of their rival Zara and the conquest of Constantinople.1 From another point of view the Venetians were a group of devout crusaders who, through a series of unfortunate events and choices, destroyed the Christian city of Zara, then sacked and conquered the Christian city of Constantinople, heart of the Byzantine Empire, and home to the Greek Church, with whom the Latin Church hoped one day to reunify.2 The truth lies somewhere between these two points of view. This paper will examine the way in which the costs of preparing for the Crusade combined with a series of unfortunate events which occurred as the crusade was underway, led to the capture and sacking of Constantinople. This will be accomplished by providing an overview of the Fourth Crusade, then examining the differing motivations between the Venetian leaders of the Crusade and their foot soldiers, followed by an examination of the economic motivations of the Crusaders once underway, and how those motivations led to the sacking of Constantinople.

*****

In 1198, with the ascension of Pope Innocent III a new crusade to retake the Holy Land was proclaimed. The plan was to sneak through Egypt because the Crusaders had realized that so long as the Muslims could raise an army from Egypt, they could retake Jerusalem.3 To fund this enterprise, the Pope called for a direct tax on the Church. This can be seen in the Pope’s writing to the Archbishop and clergy of Magdeburg that they needed “to render at least a fortieth part of all his ecclesiastical revenues in money and in kind for the aid of the Holy Land.”4 This was especially impactful on the Venetians, to whom Pope Innocent also wrote, calling for an end to the Venetians’ trade with Egypt, and to instead turn their resources to helping the Holy Land, and to keep suffering through the losses in trade revenue.5

The Doge of Venice negotiated with the representatives of the Crusaders, agreeing to transport 4,500 horses and knights, 9,000 esquires, and 20,000 foot soldiers, for a fee of 85,000 marks.6 To transport the knights and horses, special ships had to be built, and to ensure there would be enough ships, trade with other ports was suspended for the summer as they waited for Crusaders to arrive, with the hope of leaving by the end of June.7 They would not set out until the start of October. Further complicating the matter, only 1,000 Knights and 50,000- or 60,000-foot soldiers arrived, well below what had been speculated, and from them only 50,000 of the agreed upon 85,000 marks could be gathered.8 To pay off the debt, the Doge of Venice proposed that the Venetians be paid off with money gathered from the first conquest en-route. However, because it was winter, they could not cross the sea to Egypt.9 Instead the Doge proposed they attack the city of Zara, because they “have done us much evil, and I and my men want to punish them, if we can … [and because] The city of Zara is very rich and well supplied with all kinds of provisions.”10 This would settle an old score, while ensuring that the army was fed and prepared for the proper Crusade come spring.

While laying siege to Zara, the townsfolk offered to surrender the city, but were convinced by some in the Crusaders’ army to give up on their plea for peace as the crusading pilgrims would not support the Venetians in their attack against a Christian city. This angered the Doge, who had been speaking to a council of barons and counts on what to do with the offer. One member of the clergy spoke against the Doge, saying “I forbid you, in the name of the pope at Rome, to attack this city; for the inhabitants are Christians and you are pilgrims.”11 The Doge pointed out that he nearly had the city until those in the army spoke against him, and now the pilgrims should do as they promised and aid the Doge in conquering the city, which they did in 5 days.12 After the city had been taken, the two armies settled in for the winter.

As summer came around, the pilgrims realized they lacked the necessary money or supplies to continue their crusade. The Doge told the crusaders that “Greece is a very rich land and bountifully supplied with everything [we need].”13 This was supported by the Frankish leader of the Crusade, Boniface of Montferrat, who told of meeting the ‘rightful’ heir to the Byzantine Empire, and that by installing the heir on the throne, the Crusaders could get the supplies and money they needed from the new Byzantine Emperor.14

The crusaders then went on and conquered Constantinople, installing Alexius III on the throne. Once on the throne, he paid the crusaders and Venetians their promised money, but failed to deliver the agreed upon supplies.15 The bishops agreed this war was a righteous one: “for the Greeks were traitors and murderers, and also disloyal, since they had murdered their rightful lord, and were worse than Jews.”16 After a failed attack, a second was launched and succeeded. With the success of the siege, the crusaders sacked and pillaged the city, raping, slaughtering, and looting.17 Holy artifacts were looted from churches, sometimes by crusaders and sometimes by churchmen seeking to protect the artifacts, altars were destroyed, and blood was spilled.18

As a result of the violence against their fellow Christians, any hope of reunifying the Greek and Latin churches was shattered. This was one of the largest of the many reasons behind the anger of the Pope, another being the absolution of crusading vows for those who were going to stay in the new Latin Empire which the Pope said was outside the authority of those priests who were with the crusaders to absolve.19

*****

In the narrative of the Fourth Crusade, the Doge of Venice, and Boniface of Montferrat played key roles in influencing the fate of the Crusade and its destinations. This highlights one of the key issues during this crusade: the difference in information between the leaders and the soldiers.

A clear example of the withholding of information can be seen in the different reasons for the groups going on Crusade, specifically the Franks and the Venetians. The Venetians believed the Crusade on Constantinople had been sanctioned by the Pope, with the hope of putting Alexius III on the throne to reunite the Greek and Latin Churches, while the Franks believed they were making a stop along the way to their actual destination of Egypt.20 In reality, Montferrat wanted to settle an old score, and put Alexis III on the throne, while the Doge wanted to get paid for the resources Venice had devoted to the Crusade.21

It is important to remember that the Venetians were not just interested in money. The foot soldiers were devout crusaders themselves and hoped to be good servants of the pope, hence why they were quick to take such dramatic actions as halting trade for months on end and devoting considerable manpower and resources to the Crusade.22 As well, due to the secrecy enforced by the Doge, the Venetians were not aware of their excommunication and the Pope’s lack of support for attacking Constantinople.23 They, like their Frankish counterparts, were wary about attacking Constantinople, but due to language barriers and intentional separation of forces, the two factions could not interact and share their misgivings of the upcoming attack.24

Thus, while the average Venetian may have been motivated by money to some degree, they joined the Crusade out of devotion to the Church and its pope.25 The Venetians’ excommunication had been held from them out of fear that the Venetians might leave the Crusade, as they had no deep desire to destroy Constantinople, which the Venetians had done a great deal of trade with, and may have instead preferred a new trade partner in a possible Crusader-controlled Egypt.26

Crusading is expensive, and it because of this fact that the Pope issued a direct tax on the Church to pay for the Fourth Crusade. In preparing for the Crusade, trade from Venice was halted, special ships to transport the horses had to be commissioned, resources were used, and men were required, all of which needed to be paid for.27 An amount had been agreed to, 85,000 marks, which according to Queller and Day was in line with costs to transport personnel during previous crusades.28 As a result of chance, the group of Crusaders who arrived in Venice was smaller than expected, and were only able to gather 50,000 marks of their fare, even after charging the Crusaders above the agreed upon rates and having used all possible lines of credit.29 The Venetians deserved to be paid for their resources and lost trade, and the Crusaders were responsible for missing the pre-agreed launch deadline. With winter set in, travel by ocean was limited, narrowing the options for the Crusaders until the choice of Zara appeared – the small fact that it was under papal protection could be ignored and justified – so it was attacked.30 After spending winter in the city, exhausting its resources, the Crusaders needed to gather more, as well as the outstanding debts owed to Venice. This economic reason, combined with a political motive from Montferrat – a personal issue with Constantinople - and a religious motive – the reunification of the two dominant Christian Churches – made for an appealing target for the Crusade’s leadership.31 The practical economic motivation is further reinforced after the installation of Alexius III as Emperor, who paid his monetary debt, but owed the Crusaders the truly important logistical element of provisions and resources. It is the failure to provide these provisions which motivated the Doge to once again attack Constantinople and allowing for it to be given to various Crusaders as their fiefs, to form the new Latin Empire.32 In the aftermath of the sack of Constantinople, the Venetians had been repaid and resupplied, the Franks got lands, and the Church got holy artifacts and the establishment of a Latin empire out of Constantinople.33

*****

War depends on men and materiel, and in the Fourth Crusade, the Crusaders had a lot of bodies, but a poor logistical network. The failure of people to arrive in Venice, combined with both parties’ need to uphold their contracts to one another, led to a series of events which resulted in the sacking of Constantinople and the foundation of the Latin Empire. While the Venetians surely profited from the Crusade, many of their soldiers were devout Catholics who went on Crusade to fight for their faith. The Doge of Venice seems to have been more motivated by money, yet this desire is at least in part motivated by wanting to ensure his people were repaid for their goods and services. However, it is because of the Doge’s desire to settle an old score with Zara, and his lying to his troops, that the Crusade was able to spiral so out of control, and it was because of economic motivations – the desire to be repaid and to have enough supplies for the Crusades – that the spiraling went as far as it did.
