A unique toleration: an examination of religious toleration in the Early Modern Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth

By 1573 Europe had been ravaged by the wars of religion – word of such horrors as the St. Bartholomew’s Days massacre spread throughout continent. In the Kingdom of Poland and the Grand Duchy of Lithuania, the shared nobles sought to protect their power, and their own religious freedom from the same fate as was met by religious minorities in France and elsewhere. To do this, the nobles of the two countries worked together to form the Warsaw Confederation. Inspired by the Peace of Augsburg, the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth would embrace religious freedom for its nobles to the degree that “if one were to look for [religious] toleration in Western Europe before the 1680s … One would look to the east: to the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth in its glory days after the Confederation of Warsaw in 1573.”1

This paper will examine the topic of religious tolerance in the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth. It will do this by examining if religious toleration was present in the Commonwealth, why this religious toleration developed in the Commonwealth, and what the role of the Catholic Church and Catholicism as a whole were in the evolution of religious toleration in the Commonwealth. However, it should be noted that the sources on this topic which are available in English are limited, which has made drawing a conclusion on the extent to which religious toleration existed and the role of the Catholic Church difficult.

In the early modern period, religious toleration was the exception and not the rule across Europe; and yet for the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, this was not the case, in large part due to the diversity of religion present in the Commonwealth at its inception. Poland had been converted to Catholicism during the reign of Duke Mieszko in the 10th century.2 The 11th century saw Jewish people becoming a permanent fixture in Poland, and by the 13th century the followers of the Jewish religion had been granted “self government, a separate legal status, and freedom of faith”.3 In the 13th century, King Casimir the Great conquered Ruthenia which had historically followed the Greek Orthodox tradition.4 He also arranged for the marriage of his daughter Jadwiga of Anjou to Prince Jogaila of Lithuania, bringing with him more Jewish people as well as many Muslims, and laying the foundation of religious diversity so important for the development of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth.5 After the Reformation had begun, Poland became known as a safe haven for those who disagreed with the Catholic Church. After the publication of Martin Luther’s Ninety-five Theses, his works were found in the largest cities of Poland, which motivated protestant groups like the Bohemian Brethren to move to Poland: “In the Kingdom of Poland, with its long borders and diverse population, Orthodox, Jews, and Muslims could live in peace as well.”6

In late January 1573, fearing the spread of the religious wars in Germany to their lands, the nobility in their Sejm (the equivalent to Parliament) enacted the Pax Dissidentium, which identified all religious groups – including Catholics – which differed in faith as dissidenties, and granted them complete legal equality with the Catholic Church.7 There was, however, a clause which gave nobles the ability to decide over their subjects in matters of religion, thus limiting the extent of religious liberties for the common person.8 This widespread religious diversity, combined with the power of the nobility, and the size of the Commonwealth made it all but inevitable that “the early [Polish-Lithuanian] state [was] forced to accept a kind of religious coexistence.”9 However, these factors still do not fully explain the course which led to the development of religious toleration as it appeared in the Confederation.

Religious toleration as it appeared in the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth was due to two major factors: the spread of Protestantism among the nobility, gentry, and middle class; and the development of the Sejm as a representative institution for the nobility.

The high point of Protestantism in Poland was around 1570, as in that period 700 places of Protestant worship existed, and of the various social classes, half of the nobility had converted to Protestantism, a quarter of the gentry and towns folk had converted, and around five percent of the peasant class had converted – meaning that Protestantism was dominated by the nobility and middle classes, while being in the minority in society.10 Most of the nobles and gentry who adopted the Protestant faith had come from Little Poland and Lithuania, though Lutheranism spread among the German speaking nobles and gentry in Greater Poland and Royal Prussia.11 When nobles went to study abroad they learned of social and religious issues leading to their conversion.12 For those of the gentry who were interested in Protestantism, the University of Krakow offered a haven for their studies.13 Those in the gentry who became interested in Protestantism reached this position due to strong beliefs in the moral degeneracy of clerics and the hypocrisy of the Catholic Church.14 Those nobles who did not convert still expressed normal medieval anti-clerical views as well as a resentment against a rival power group in the Catholic Church.15

As Protestantism spread, it began to have an effect on the royal finances, leading to “[King Sigismund Augustus]’s policy of allowing full freedom of religion to Lutherans in Danzig, Thorn, and Elbing, in 1557/58, [which] was predicated neither upon altruism nor religious relativism, but rather upon the king’s financial need.”16 However, during the 1560s Protestantism would also begin its fall, as it lost the nine theological and noble people who acted as pillars of support for the Protestant faith in Poland.17 However, this decline would not become visible for decades, and Protestantism was, at the dawn of the 1570s, a dominant power.18 The Polish Brethren, Lutherans and The Reformed made an attempt to join together, and while nothing would come of that union, it laid the foundation for the creation of the Warsaw Confederation three years later in 1573.19 As the years went on, the greatest continued supporters of Protestantism would be the gentry and middle class in Greater Poland.20

The spread of Protestantism is just one piece of the development of religious toleration; the other much more important piece is the politics of the nobility, and how they used the assurance of religious toleration – among other clauses – as a secondary assurance of noble authority over the King. The first steps for the development of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth’s elected kingship and Sejm can be found in the reign of King Casimir the Great, who lacked his own male heir and chose to allow the nobility to have a voice in determining his successor.21 The groups agreed to the oldest daughter of the King of Hungary, Louis of Anjou; following the death of King Casimir, the nobility instead chose to accept a younger daughter of Louis’ Jadwiga, who then married Jagillo Duke of Lithuania and who was later recognized as King of Poland, and who would extend the rights and prerogatives of the Polish nobility to the nobles in Lithuania.22 Over the next century the nobility would continue to expand their rights until they had gained control over the entirety of the legislative branch; these rights applied “whether rich or poor, landed or landless, rural or urban, all nobles were legally the same.”23 This allowed the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth to have the largest political class in Europe at that time, composed of six to seven percent of the population.24

This large political class expressed its desire for religious liberty in 1552 during a Sejm, during which the nobility questioned the authority of the bishops, deciding that: “no one but the monarch had the right to judge citizen and to condemn them to any penalty what so ever,” eventually agreeing that the clergy only had power in matters of religion, and none in civil or political life.25 This was expanded on during a Sejm in 1556 to guarantee everyone the right to worship as one wished.26 By the late 1550s the Protestants nearly had a majority in the Sejm, however it was split among various Protestant sects.27 One of the large draws of Protestantism for poor nobles who lacked land was to act as “a powerful weapon in [their] pursuit against the privilege and position of the kings, the magnates, and the higher clergy.”28 This is why the agreement made between various Protestant groups in 1570 was important: it allowed the groups to create a coalition in the Sejm and in 1573 pass the Pax Dissidentium, and by extension create the Pacta Conventa which Henry of Valois agreed to and which granted religious toleration to the subjects of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth.29

The role of the Catholic Church in the development of religious toleration can best be described as antagonistic – the Church was not happy about losing its hold over the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth. This was not practical for the rulers of Poland however, as: “the weakness of royal authority and the political realism of Sigismund the old – and eventually of Sigismund Augustus – led polish rulers to conclude that social order was of higher value than religious unity” which made it “clear that, had the power of the Church within the polity or the authority of royal rule within the kingdom been sufficient and greater, dissent, heterodoxy, heresy, and Christian diversity would not have been tolerated to the degree they were.”30 However that is not to say that the Church and Kings did not try. For example King Sigismund the Old was staunchly Catholic, supported Catholic Church reform, and was deeply hostile to the Reformation, going so far as to ban importation of Luther’s books under threat of confiscation and banishment.31 However, he was unwilling to arrest and execute heretics even when urged to do so by a papal envoy.32 His son, King Sigismund II Augustus cared little for religion, but was a conservative and traditionalist, which kept him in the Catholic faith, but said of others’ religions: “I am not the king of your consciences,” and it was during his reign that Protestantism reached its peak in the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth.33 As a result of King Sigismund Augustus’ toleration, Henry of Valois was forced to sign the Pacta Conventa, which was “an international treaty concluded between Charles IX and the Polish and Lithuanian Estates; it was an agreement between the new king and his subjects, wherein certain obligations made by Henry were enumerated”, including religious toleration.34 However, the Warsaw Confederation’s religious tolerations were weak in application due to Catholic resistance, and its protections of freedom of religion would last for around a century as a result of class solidarity.35

Class solidary from the nobility would not prevent the Church from “[unleashing] a strong counter-campaign excommunicating many clergy and noblemen, condemning them to death and confiscating their property.”36 Rome opposed and censured the Pax dissidentium, and the Catholic Church’s primary supporter, Cardinal Stanislaus Hosius, recommended that King Henry of Valois retract his oath of religious tolerance, and invited the Jesuits to Poland.37 The Jesuits would truly begin their anti-protestant campaign after the end of Henry de Valois time reigning over Poland.38 To achieve these ends, they used “brute physical force, gaining control over education, destroying the concept of law and individual rights, replacing them with prejudice and corruption, and supporting aggression against Protestants.”39 However, the most common methods used to convert people were: “giving talks that extolled study in catholic Academic Centers, using wives and friends to apply pressure, and encouraging new converts to influence others,” and the most important reason for peoples’ conversion was “fear of pulling the commonwealth into the religious wars, a lack of strong political groupings in Polish dissent circles, the lack of widespread support for the slogans of the Reformation, disillusionment with the never-ending conflicts, and opportunism.”40 These issues, combined with the actions of the Jesuits meant that in the latter half of the 17th century, the Catholic Church was able to gain greater control over the country, preventing departures from the faith under threat of expulsion from the country.41 By “1717, the last non-Catholics were removed from all offices and titles.”42

The Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth was a unique state in the early modern period, as a result of its large size and the way in which it had grown to be contain a religiously diverse population. As well, due to several instances where kings were left without male heirs, the nobility was able to gain control over the legislative branch of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth’s government, and due to their desire to hold onto power, they enacted protections for religious freedoms. However, as class solidarity declined, the Catholic Church was able to convert Protestants to Catholicism, and as the Catholics, supported by the Jesuits, gained power, the now Catholic-dominated nobility acted to remove the religious liberties that their predecessors had fought so hard to attain.
