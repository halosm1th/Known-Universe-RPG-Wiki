The Cuban Revolution was a radical transformation of Cuban society, which was able to “[achieve] remarkably high health, education, and other socioeconomic indicators that rival those of industrialized countries.”1 However, the revolution was not without its limits, a few of the key areas being: economic troubles, disparities along racial and gender lines, and the housing and food crises.

This paper will examine the key areas of success from the Cuban Revolution, those of healthcare, education, and spreading revolutionary ideas, as well as examining the key limitations of the revolution: income distribution, race and gender issues, and housing and food allocation.

The first area of success of the revolution that will be examined is healthcare. As discussed by Becker, after the Revolution the healthcare system underwent a boom, seeing a tenfold budget increase and free healthcare for all. This, combined with increases in education, meant the country was able to have a rate of one doctor per two hundred people – and half those doctors were women.2 Cuba’s medical miracle resulted in Cuba having the highest density of doctors in Latin America, a fact made more impressive because “there were only about 7,000 doctors in the country [who] were primarily employed in the private sector, and one third of them left the country during the first years of the revolution.”3 This led to Cuba becoming a leader in biotechnical research and medicine, while also assuming the role of a hospital for the rest of Latin America, a place where those who could not get treatment elsewhere could turn to for aid.4 Doctors also became key to Cuba’s foreign policy goals, as doctors were sent to Venezuela for oil, and to other countries in Latin America in exchange for foreign currency.5 However, the healthcare system was not perfect, and the US embargo prevented certain basic medicines from being readily accessible. As well, the Special Period in the 1990s led to the deterioration of hospital infrastructure. 6

The medical boom was helped because “the revolutionaries quickly built a strong education system.”7 The investment in education saw a boost in literacy levels “from 76 percent before the revolution to 96 percent in 1962, the highest in Latin America and a level that equaled that of wealthy, industrialized countries.”8 Additionally, by 1961 all schools in Cuba had been made free.9 A primary goal of the changes was raising the education level of and further integrating rural areas and women into Cuban society.10 This was a success, as practically all children up to the age of 17 were in school, and the secondary education completion rate rose from 3.5 percent in 1953 to 38.7 in 1986.11 Through the turbulent 1990s, women represented 60 percent of enrollment in higher education.12 The Revolution had also ensured that blacks and mulattos “had roughly similar access to … education.”13 This focus on education remained even after the economic slowdown began in the 1990s, when a program was started which would “[have students] start working as social workers with the possibility of studying at university at the same time.”14 This led to the popularization of the idea of the “universalization of higher education.”15

The Revolution was committed to its revolutionary ideals and worked to export them into the world. Becker states that “Cuba pursued an internationalist foreign policy,”16 fighting with anticolonialist movements in Africa, while inspiring revolutionary movements closer to home.17 Most notably in its international efforts was the devotion of troops to help Angola fight against South Africa, demonstrating that the better armed white forces could be defeated.18

Cuba pursued this policy for two reasons: first it helped them gain allies abroad while demonstrating Cuban resilience and shaming the United States, and second it helped in demonstrating the government’s commitment to revolutionary ideas. The first reason is especially important, as those allies helped prevent an American invasion of the socialist island nation, and made the United States appear weak.19 The second reason is visible in the opinion of Cuba and Moscow on one another: Castro had no issue removing those loyal to Moscow from his government and following economic policies which benefited Cuba to the detriment of the USSR, and the USSR who “muttered about pouring funds down the Cuban rathole.”20 This devotion to revolutionary spirit was recognized by Henry Kissinger, who described Castro as: “probably the most genuine revolutionary leader then in power.”21

However, even the strongest revolutionary spirits run into limitations imposed by reality. The Revolution succeeded in reducing inequality in income distribution due to the Soviet Union buying Cuban sugar for above market prices.22 Following the collapse of the Soviet Union, income inequality has started to creep back in. There are several reasons for this: family members living abroad who send money to their family back in Cuba, racism in the tourism industry; and investment in certain new areas of industry which get access to government investment.23

The rise in income inequality has exposed the limitations of the Revolution with regards to race and gender issues. It was officially declared that Cuba was without racism, however “international observers agree that most tourist-related jobs are performed by individuals deemed to be white in Cuba.”24 This is “due to aesthetic considerations and to the alleged preferences of the tourists.”25 As well, the majority of exiles from Cuba are white, which in turn means that a majority of remittances are from white families living abroad. These two factors push Afro-Cubans “to activities that are perceived as either illegal or unethical in order to access the dollar economy.”26

A second set of limitations exist for women who like those in the West, hit a glass ceiling in their jobs, which exacerbated inequalities during the Special Period.27 Attempts to legislate equality in the household, through things like the Family Code of 1975, have seen some success, especially thanks to their granting of near equality of consensual union and legal marriage, and regulating an even split of household chores between the family.28 However, the greatest drivers of inequality and patriarchy are in the allocation of housing and food.

Cuba has struggled to build housing for its revolutionary population but there has been great progress made in modernizing the existing homes – bringing in utilities like plumbing and electricity.29 But, the lack of housing, and the corruption in the housing system has led to the rise of shanty towns, and as income inequality increased during the Special Period, so too did the inequality between the genders for those living in the shanty towns – women spent less time working in formal jobs, instead taking care of the home.30

The greatest limitation in Cuba is that of food. Due to the dependency on the USSR paying above market rates for Cuban sugar exports, its collapse hurt the economy of Cuba, leading to the Special Period.31 Without money from the USSR, Cuba was unable to buy food, and had to implement even more restrictive rationing; rationing had been in effect in Cuba since 1961 but had failed in alleviating the food issues.32 There is hope however for the future: in 2008 the government introduced new land reform laws which allowed “farmers and cooperatives access to idle land ... to increase production.”33

If one measures Cuba by where it has come from, the Revolution has seen many great successes: healthcare, education, equalizing of society, and the spreading of revolutionary ideas. However, the limits of these successes are visible and put strain on Cuba, such as the inequities of income distribution and its racialized and gendered impacts, as well as the limitations of housing reform due to corruption, and the food allocation challenges following the collapse of the country’s largest trading partner.
