At the end of the 17th century, following the Glorious Revolution in England, another, arguably more important, revolution was beginning to take hold: the financial revolution, which would bring with it cheap and less restricted credit, allowing the English economy to grow into a powerhouse. This revolution was based on the tools and systems which arose in the Middle Ages in response to usury laws. The evolution of usury and credit into modern financial instruments is explored through the following three articles: “An Economic Model for the Medieval Church: Usury as a Form of Rent Seeking” by Robert Ekelund, et al., “Money, Banking, and Credit in Medieval Bruges” by Raymond de Roover, and lastly John H. Munro’s “The Medieval Origins of the Financial Revolution: Usury, Rentes, and Negotiability.”

This paper examines each article, starting with an explanation of the thesis of the article, followed by a summary of the article’s contents, and finally examining the evidence given for each article: what sources and arguments does each article use to support its thesis. After examining the articles individually, the similarities between the articles will then be discussed, highlighting the eventual development of credit, and the way that these medieval institutions are still visible in the world today.

The first article is “An Economic Model for the Medieval Church: Usury as a Form of Rent Seeking” by Robert Ekelund, Robert Hebert, and Robert Tollison. The thesis of the article is that the Catholic Church had a monopoly on offering salvation to the people of Western Europe, and that the Church tried to maintain that monopoly using a combination of doctrinal purity, regulations against certain practices, and corporeal agents.1 The article then demonstrates its thesis using usury as an example. The article details the Church’s relationship to usury laws, explaining how various councils and popes influenced the development of the cannon concept of usury.2 The article then provides an explanation of how the Church had a “pattern of select enforcement” around the usury doctrine, enforcing it only when “it was in the Church’s interest to do so.”3 One such example is the Medicis, whose usurious activities the Church ignored, while supporting the banking family in other ways, such as by making them the exclusive agents in overseeing the production from a major aluminium mine, then working with the Medicis to get rid of other competition in that industry.4 The article then summarizes the Church’s position in the loan market as “operat[ing] on both sides,” borrowing freely from its bankers, offering usurious loans in private, all “while outwardly declaring public usurers anathema.”5

The primary sources used in the article are Vatican records, as well as other official documentation from the Church. However, as noted in the article, the conclusions reached are those of trained economists, and not theologians. A result of this is that an economist tends to look for economic answers, which the article highlights by offering two possible hypotheses: one spiritual and one economic. The two hypotheses are referred to throughout the article, though the dominant view is the economic view, and the spiritual view is merely offered without much analysis. A variety of secondary sources are used throughout the article as well, with two reoccurring names being Nelson and Lunt, though others come up as well to add further support to the topic, including the author whose article will be discussed next: De Roover.

“Money, Banking, and Credit in Medieval Bruges” by Raymond de Roover explains “the three different classes of money-dealers: the Italian merchant-bankers, the lombards, and the money-changers.”6 The article’s thesis is that credit played a vital role in the medieval economy, and that the three classes of money dealers each found their own ways to subvert usury laws, thus turning a profit on interest. The article makes clear that these money-dealers did not arise until the 13th century, and the pre-13th century loans were generally done by monasteries.7 The article then goes on to explain how the Italian bankers got started in Bruges, (having been granted trade privileges), and explaining the structures of two competing banks, before finally detailing the use of bills of exchange, and the way in which the bill could return a profit for the banker. A bill of exchange was a way to transfer money between cities without having to physically transport gold, while avoiding usury laws by not knowing when the banker would be paid, and by using differences in currencies’ exchange rates to further enhance the bankers’ profit.8 The next group examined are the pawnbrokers, known as lombards, who rather than finding a crafty way to avoid the negative connotations of being a manifester of usury, chose instead to embrace their title and get special privileges to “lend “money for more money.””9 The last group looked at in this article is that of the money-changers. The first important distinction De Roover notes for the money changers is in their status as citizens and thus their eligibility for municipal offices. The second important distinction was that their position was seen as a “quasi-public function,” due to the requirement that they take an oath of office and follow monetary ordinances.10 The article then details the way that the money-changers made money: by offering a bank account where people could deposit bullion that the bank could loan out; by acting as the go between from the mint to the people; and by facilitating payment in the form of bank deposits, which effectively acted as a bank note.11

The evidence for “Money, Banking, and Credit in Medieval Bruges” is officially none, as the only citation in the paper says that it contains no references to sources,”12 but that the author will give the facts and supporting evidence in a more extensive study that was set to come out in the future. However, the author does reference different primary sources that while uncited, are the basis of much of the article’s argument. For the Italian bankers, the article details the way in which the bill of exchange was used to earn a profit, effectively using the conceptual bill of exchange and the logic on which it operated as the primary source for this section. The pawnbrokers primary source is the license they were granted to conduct their trade, as well as the receipts that they gave to those who pawned an item.13 The money-changers have the most detailed sources, as Bruges’ archive had the account books of two different changers. These account books allowed De Roover to examine and explain various facets of the money-changers trade, including the use of deposits as transferable currency, the relationship between changer and mint, and the probability that one in thirty-five to forty people may have had a bank account.14 Further evidence is provided for these conclusions through the use of a map of the city, which De Roover makes several references to, explaining the relationship between location and the industries which the various credit-extending groups assisted. Interestingly, one other conclusion reached through the map is in the difference between the pawnbroker and money-changer. De Roover explains that the pawnbroker needed a larger house to store the various pawned goods, while a money-changer would have lost trust had they entered the pawning business, and thus only needed a relatively small house.

The third article is “The Medieval Origins of the Financial Revolution: Usury, Rentes, and Negotiability” by John H. Munro. The thesis of this article is following the interplay between rentes and usury through the Middle Ages into the late 17th century. The article details how usury laws pushed rentes to evolve over time in various forms in the future Dutch Republic, starting with an explanation of the history of usury laws, the distinction between rentes which were licit and those which were usurious.15 The article goes on to explain how the Flemish had two forms of rentes: a perpetual hereditary rent, and a newer life-rent.16 These two rentes would be the basis of future investment tools for municipal and eventually national governments. The article then details the rise of banking in the Low Countries, and the issuing of Rentes to fund municipal actions leading to the creation of secondary financial markets.17 The next key development is in negotiability. This development was brought on by the English, who had a strong royal monopoly on money changing and trade in bullion, which in turn prevented England from developing private bank deposits like those in Flanders until the 1640s.18 This change accelerated following the creation of Lex Mercantoria around the 1280s.19 The Law Merchant created a shared legal system between the Dutch Republic and Britain for trade, which was helpful given the two’s economic interdependence.20 Then, following the Glorious Revolution, William of Orange brought with him a system of long-term borrowing which the English state could adopt.21 The article then explains how the debts created by this system were consolidated “into the Consolidated Stock of the Nation, popularly known as Consols.”22 The paper ends by explaining the continued existence of Consols into the 21st century, and the optimistic observation that: “the responses to the prohibition of usury promoted rather than retarded European economic progress.”23

Munro uses many sources in comparison to the previous two articles, with every page having at least one, if not many more, sources to back up the arguments presented. The article does little of its own arguments, acting more as a summary of the financial revolution than an argument about any specific part of the revolution. The secondary sources include references to De Roover’s work, including “Money, Banking, and Credit in Medieval Bruges” when Munro explains the way that a bill of exchange worked. Most of the secondary sources were published after the second World War, with some being published as recently as 2001 and others being published in the mid 19th century. The article builds on a variety of primary sources, from papal edicts to town record, as well as other primary sources from the 12th century onwards to explain the financial revolution.

In comparing the three articles, two themes emerge: that the Church’s belief in usury was only an impediment in the development of credit, one for which work arounds could be found, and that as a result, the foundation of the modern financial system can be found in the Middle Ages.

The key area on which all three articles agree is that the Church’s ban on usury did not stop the development of tools for credit, it only slowed down their development for the common person. In “An Economic Model” it is explained how the Church exploited their monopoly on salvation to selectively enforce usury laws, allowing the Church to benefit from credit during periods like the Crusades while also allowing the Church to restrict the growth of competition by preventing competition from getting credit.24 In effect the Church did not prevent the development of economic tools; it merely attempted to monopolize their existence. This monopoly was shown to be unsuccessful by de Roover in “Money, Banking, and Credit in Medieval Bruges,” as de Roover lays out three different ways in which non-church members were able to create credit, whether through bills of exchange, outright usurious pawn brokering, or through the bullion trade. De Roover’s analysis ends by confirming the vitality of credit to the medieval commercial centers, and thus the Church’s failure to enforce its monopoly.25 Finally, Munro’s work is on the whole supportive of the concept of the slowdown, in its earliest pages working to dispel the myth “that the ban (on usury) had no effect on medieval trade and finance”26 by spending the rest of the article discussing the evolution that medieval trade and finance undertook to get around the ban on usury.

As a result, all three articles demonstrate the eventual shift from usury to credit, and in doing so, each article demonstrates how their chosen topic has ramifications into the modern world. In “An Economic Model”, it is made clear by applying the modern concept of rent seeking behavior to the medieval practice of usury, as well as explaining how through the developments of the eighth and ninth centuries, the medieval Church created a series of institutions which resemble practices in the modern business world, to maintain their monopoly on salvation.27 De Roover is more explicit in the connection between the medieval and modern worlds, saying: “It is therefore not true that the failure to invent the bank note hampered the development of capitalism in the Middle Ages,”28 as bank deposits performed the same function as bank notes. Munro makes this point, that the lack of bank note did not hamper the development of capitalism. Munro does this by explicitly tracing the development of capitalism from the early Rentes to the eventual development of the British Consols, and that the bill of exchange is not gone, it has instead evolved into “international ‘acceptances’” that are essential for modern commerce and finance.29

De Roover’s work is detailed and specific, focusing on one area, and one concept, but examining each part of that concept with some, ultimately limited, depth. Ekelund et al. are broader in their study, approaching the broad concept of the Church’s monopoly on salvation and then applying that monopoly to a single area to view its effects. In doing so, Ekelund expands on the circumstances in which de Roover’s money-dealers operated. The connecting piece for the two is in Munro’s analysis. Taking a large overview of a wide period across a wide area, Munro gives the broad stroke analysis of key events in the evolution from the medieval economic system to the modern one. This overview allows for de Roover’s money-dealers to be put into context with Ekelund’s monopolistic Church relative to the overall development of financial systems, as the money-dealers become a necessary piece in subverting the Church’s monopoly through an evolving legal understanding of usury.
