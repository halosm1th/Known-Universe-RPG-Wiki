One system to organize and integrate the readings covered during the Winter Semester would be following the economic evolution of Latin America during the 20th century, subsequent to the Great Depression. This would allow reader to gain a deeper understanding of the economic impacts on the governments of Latin American counties, the rise and impact of US economic imperialism on Latin America, and the effects of the economic changes to various social groups. In essence it would argue that the Great Depression laid a foundation which was built on by the Cold War which created the conditions that the United States could use to further exploit and effectively colonize the countries of Latin America. As well, it would demonstrate that not even communist Cuba could escape the economic imperialism of the United States and the resulting inequality and social issues that went with it.

This organizational system is at its core economic, tracing the shift from import substitution to neoliberalism. However, to explore the impact of economic changes, it would also include social and political history. This allows the reader to develop an understanding of the relationship between a nation’s government, its people, and its economy. As well, this system would demonstrate the relationship between the economic imperialism of the United States, and its impact on those living in the colonized areas, as well as the transnational nature of Latin American history.

This paper will provide a broad overview of each of the readings in this semester in the order they were read, discussing the core theme of each, and how each would fit into this system of organization. Then it will explain how the readings should be structured, why it should be structured in that manner, and the benefits that manner of organization offers.

The readings on the Great Depression explore the impacts of the Great Depression on Latin America, arguing that South America’s experience of the Great Depression was better than most countries in the developed world, such as the United States.1 This is because this period saw the shift in economic policy towards Import Substitution Industrialization (ISI), which saw an expansion in exports, growth of the middle class, and a rise in government control and involvement in the economy.2 At the same time, the articles discuss the impact of the depression on the working class, for whom it was much worse than the middle or upper class – who did still experience discomfort but to a lesser extent – leading to an increase in radicalized people growing increasingly disenchanted with their government, the wealthy land owners, and other elites.3 The articles explain that countries like Mexico saw a rise in labor rights and land redistribution during this period, demonstrating the impacts of the Depression in the day to day lives of those living outside of urban society.4 A secondary result of the Depression was a rise in urbanization, as well as a rise in foreign ownership of capital intensive industries, such as oil or the international fruit trade. This is because oil required a large amount of capital to find and extract the oil, and bananas require ships which could deliver on time to the United States. As a result of the Great Depression, American companies were able to enter countries like Colombia and use their position to consolidate control over oil fields, and shipping fruit, setting an early foundation for the economic imperialism of the United States.5 Finally, the readings on the Great Depression also explore the racialized and gendered impact of the Great Depression, establishing a baseline against which future developments in those areas can be judged.6

The next reading to look at is the reading on dictatorship and power in the Caribbean. In The Dictators Seduction this system would examine the impact of the rise of the Trujillo, following the invasion of the Dominican Republic by the United States, to their eventual leaving of the island, which in turn gave the American marine-trained soldier Trujillo an opening to seize power.7 At roughly the same time, the first version of American post Great Depression economic imperialism was introduced. This system was established when the United States left the island but it continued to flood the island with cheap duty-free products, as opposed to the common shift in the Caribbean and Latin America to ISI.8

Examining Trujillo’s regime also acts as an early demonstration of what would come in other Latin American countries: things such as the repression of women, large scale state surveillance turning neighbor on neighbor, with fear enforced by a secrete police force creating an environment where distrust was common.9 Another theme which the Trujillo years highlight is the relationship of race in the Caribbean and South America, both in its vague, socially defined composition, and in the impact it made on people’s day to day lives. This can be seen in the article on the Hattian Massacre, which demonstrates the way violence was used to create racism, the relationship between race and class, and the way elites in Latin America used racism as a justification for horrible events.10 As well, by examining the Tigueraje archetype, other leaders and nations’ gender structures can be compared and contrasted against it.11 For example, the Tiguearaje archetype’s depicts as a positive symbol of masculinity the conquest of women, something which would shift in Brazil and Argentina, the former as rebellious sexually liberated resistance fighters and the latter as a repressive pro family regime.

Examining another island in the Caribbean, the readings on Cuba provide a very different point of view to which the rest of the readings may be contrasted against. This is because from the United States’ perspective, Cuba was a threat just off their coast who defied American imperialism by resisting the aggressive actions of the United States, thus acting as a symbol of defiance which other nations could rally to instead of the United States. An example of Cuba as a contrasting view to other Latin American countries can be seen in the treatment of women, where countries like the Dominican Republic sought to subjugate their female citizens, and the southern cone sought to control and protect them, Cuba tried to equalize them by requiring by law that household labor be split equally, which saw some success in eroding hierarchies which stood in other Latin American countries.12 However, the special period has seen a rise in prostitution and general inequality.13 As well, the readings demonstrate one path that a country which broke away from the United States could take, while still being unable to fully escape the gravitational pull of the free market backed by the United States without joining another, further away, imperialist power in the Soviet Union.14 In turn, as Cuba drifted from the American sphere of influence, leaders in the United States became “[determined] to punish Cuba for the transgressions of Fidel Castro,”15 and by extension any country which tried to follow in its footsteps.16 Finally, as Latin America enters its neoliberal period, the transnational nature of Latin America can be demonstrated using Cuba, which saw a rise in race-based issues during the country’s special period, as businesses which worked with tourists preferred lighter skinned employees.17

Return to continental America, the readings on Latin America and the Cold War examine the impact of the Cold War on Latin America, demonstrating the reaction of the United States and the first steps in its attempt to shore up support for itself within its sphere of influence.18 To ensure that the United States maintained control in Latin America, the CIA carried out ‘psy ops’ and trained supportive regimes in how to prevent communists from taking over the various Latin American countries, sometimes surpassing their supposed ‘arms length’ assistance.19 An example of this is Project Pedro in Mexico, which was an attempt to show American propaganda as news reels, which ultimately failed due to funding issues, and failed to achieve its goal, because it had to be made in line with local cultural understandings and beliefs.20 As well, in the article looking at Brazil, the role of race and gender are discussed, because the media created the image of Bonnie and Clyde, turning blonde women into symbols of resistance.21 At the same time, a more liberal view of sex was taking hold among those rebelling against the Brazilian government, with a popular belief being that resistance women were themselves more liberal in their promiscuity.22

From the cultural Cold War, the readings move to the rise of the military dictatorships themselves. Jerry Davila’s Dictatorships in South America follow the rise of three southern cone dictatorships: Argentina, Brazil, and Chile from the rise of the dictatorships to their eventual fall. One theme which is highlighted with this reading is the role economics had to play in the dictatorships, on the recovery of which the dictatorships rested their legitimacy, embracing the American-backed free market ideology, and support from the American government in removing perceived subservices and those who challenged the American-backed free market dictatorships.23 The book also examines the human impact of these regimes, the disappearances and surveillance states which they created, the silent suffering and fear being disappeared while living under those regimes.24 As well, the book examines Chile, whose extreme embrace of the Free Market would be used as an example and justification for other nations to follow suit. By examining Chile specifically, the book is able to demonstrate the difference between the previous socialist leader, removed from power by the United States, and the face of the free market, Pinochet afterwards. In doing so, this reading demonstrates the impact Cuba had on American foreign policy and the fear of another country turning away from the United States towards socialism.25

Destape explores the return of democracy, and how in Argentina, after the paternalistic Junta fell, there was an explosion of sexual content and sexology everywhere in the average person’s life. In doing so, the book demonstrates the success of the United States over the Soviet Union in Latin America, as democracy came to be expressed through the purchasing of sexual content. This reaffirmed the neoliberal idea that democracy, freedom, and the free market go hand in hand.26 However the main argument of the book is that the destape was a period where people fought for and articulated their sexual rights in a more open environment. This demonstrates the shift in the popular understanding of sex and the family, as men no longer feel required to protect their wives and daughters from other men.27 This also demonstrates one of the positive changes resulting from the decline and end of the Cold War, and shift from the United States-backed repressive regime to a more open, United States supported, neoliberal democracy, towards a more open society.28 However, the book also demonstrates the limits of this newfound openness, as it was limited to heterosexual people – it primarily featured the sexualization of women for men - and as such did little to advance the acceptance of LGBTQ+ groups within Argentinian society.29

The readings on democratization and human rights explore the downfall of the repressive regimes and the struggle between different groups over whose memory of what happened gets recorded. This generally meant starting a Truth Commission with the goal of determining what happened during the dictatorships, who was involved and who needs to be held accountable.30 The articles explore the difficult social impact of the disappearances, and holding those responsible accountable, as in Argentina where there developed a difference between the regime’s officers and those following orders, challenging the idea from Nuremburg that ‘just following orders’ is not a valid defense for all involved in the atrocities in Argentina.31

The final set of readings complete the picture of the American neoliberal conquest of Latin America. Empire’s Workshop traces the rise of the United States as an imperial power forged in Latin America based on neoliberalism, from the New Deal to the early 21st century.32 By following the rise of the New Right, first created by the backlash from the defeat in Vietnam, popularized with Reagan, and pushed to the extreme by Bush, Empire’s Workshop offers a reflective look back at what was covered in the course from the American point of view.33 The end result of this is the widespread push through the IMF and acceptance of the American neoliberal ideology in Latin America.34 The failure of this is explored in two articles, which demonstrate the rise in inequality which accompanied the neoliberal period.35 The racial impact of neoliberalism is demonstrated by the fact that on average white people came to live longer, with better access to healthcare and education increasing the chances of a positive outcome and was compounded on because those with an education were able to emigrate to another country and remit money to their families at home.36 There was also a gender divided which is explored in these readings, though the outcomes here are much more closely linked to class, and location. That is, predominantly urban upper- and middle-class women were generally better educated and able to enter the white-collar work force then their rural working-class counterparts.37

This proposed system of organization has several advantages, most importantly its focus on the relationship between the economy, governments, and those impacted by these two factors, on the basis of class, gender, race, or sexuality. It also demonstrates the different forms of American imperialism, and the role it has played in the development of the various Latin American countries examined throughout these readings. For example, the United States abandonment of the Dominican Republic leading to the rise of Trujillo, who was trained by American marines, and similarities to the various governments supported with CIA training after the 1960s. As well, the differences between regimes, such as Brazil’s limited following of advice from the United States compared to Argentina or Chile.

In order to best achieve this result, I propose the following ordering of the readings. I would begin with the readings on the Great Depression, as it offers an introduction to the area, and allows the reader to gain an understanding of how the region develops over the course of the Cold War. I would then follow this up with Empire’s Workshop, whose broad overview provides a timeline to which the other readings can be pegged, allowing the reader to better follow along with the various developments explored in other readings, as well as bringing the role of American economic imperialism front and center. After Empire’s Workshop would come the readings on Cuba, as this offers a contrasting perspective to the American objective in Latin America. As well, it serves as an alternative angle from which the rest of the readings can be viewed – one angle is the imposition of the American neoliberal system onto Latin American countries, another is Latin American countries fighting for the right to define their own path forward. These readings, combined with Empire’s Workshop, build on the themes established in the Great Depression readings by showing the developments which shifted many countries from ISI towards an American free market approach, and the reason for the violent opposition to shifts towards neoliberalism. Before fully diving into the cold war, I would then read The Dictator’s Seduction, as it showcases the imperialist history of the United States in Latin America and the Caribbean, and the development of many similar tactics to what would later be seen in the southern cone during the Cold War, offering another point of comparison, both against the revolution in Cuba which went an entirely different direction. Here is an area where Empire’s Workshop would provide value, as it offers the American argument for why they went after Cuba but mostly ignored the Dominican Republic – because Cuba was communist and the Dominican Republic friendly to the United States.

With the foundation laid by the previous readings, I would then read the works on the cultural Cold War, as they demonstrate the day-to-day impact of the Cold War in people’s lives, as the effect of things like the Cuban Revolution had on people in other counties. As well, it demonstrates that the goal of the United States was not just economic control, but complete economic, political, social, and military control over the various southern cone regions in a vain attempt to keep communism out. The impacts of the cultural cold war are explored in the reading on the various dictatorships in Latin America, wherein the rhetoric put forward by the United States met the reality of life in Latin America. In this section I would again contrast these readings with both the readings on the Dominican Republic, and Empire’s Workshop. The former demonstrates the similarities and differences between the governing regimes, and the effect of active support from the United States in the Southern Cone, as opposed to the dispassionate trade view seen in the Dominican Republic. Building on this reading would be the readings on democracy and the fall of the regimes. This shows the victory of the American neoliberal agenda in breaking down popular resistance, as many countries would come to be pushed by the IMF into embracing neoliberal policies, opening the countries’ now privatized industries to investment from the companies of the United States. As well, the struggles over how to remember what had occurred during the regimes’ continuing battles established during the cultural cold war. It also shows the importance of universal human rights, both in removing the regimes and in recovering from their impact on day-to-day life. Destape would be used as a case study to demonstrate the impacts of this transition, as it demonstrates the merging of economic and democratic ideas, as well as the fight for human rights by various groups in Argentina, and the societal transformation which occurred during the Destape because of the end of the dictatorship. Finally, I would end with the readings on the impact of neoliberalism, showing the impact of the corporate imperialism of the United States on the various Latin American countries. In this I would include the readings on race and inequality in Cuba. This is because it shows that with the victory of the United States in the Cold War, no country in Latin America could escape the effects of neoliberalism, as seen by Cuba’s rising inequality and racial issues.

There are many ways the readings covered in the Winter Semester could be organized. This paper argued that one such way would be as an exploration of American economic imperialism, through the changes in economic systems in Latin America over the course of the 20th century, and examining the themes of economic, political, and social impact of these changes as they evolved over time. As well, the proposed method of organization offers two different points of view from which various readings can be related back to, so as to explore the aforementioned themes by comparing and contrasting different countries, periods, and events.
